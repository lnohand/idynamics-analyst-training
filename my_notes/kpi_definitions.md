**Revenue & Size**

* **Closing MRR** — Total recurring revenue at end of period, normalized to a monthly figure  
* **Active Customers** — Number of paying subscribers at period close  
* **ARPA** — Average Revenue Per Account (MRR ÷ active customers)  
* **Gross Margin** — Revenue minus cost of goods sold, as a % of revenue

**Retention**

GRR & NRR: GRR and NRR both measure retention but **GRR ignores expansion from existing customers, so it can never go above 100%.** NRR includes expansion, so it can. In my example: starting with $10K, losing $1,500 to churn and contraction, gaining $2K from expansion — GRR is 85% (counts only the losses), NRR is 105% (counts the $2K expansion too). NRR is higher. The reason the difference matters: **GRR tells you if you're leaking revenue from your existing base. NRR tells you if your existing customers are growing**. A company with 85% GRR but 105% NRR has a churn problem that expansion is hiding. You want both numbers on the same slide.

**ARR:** Annual Recurring Revenue, equal to MRR \* 12, the amount of recurring revenue normalized yearly. 

**Quick Ratio:** (New \+ Expansion MRR) / (Churned \+ Cancelled MRR), showcases the quality of company growth and how much bigger the new \+ expansion MRR is than the churned \+ cancelled MRR

**S\&M:** The actual amount spent on sales & marketing per period

**CAC:** Customer Acquisition Cost, the amount spent to acquire a customer, includes marketing \+ travel \+ the various expenses required to get a customer. Formula is Sales & Marketing / Number of Customers. 

The following 3 metrics are quarterly only because they become misleading over short periods of time. Realizing revenue takes time, Churn is statistically unstable from month to month, and Sales and Marketing spend happens unevenly.

**LTV:** Lifetime Value, the amount of revenue generated by a customer in the lifetime of their subscription. ARPA ÷ churn rate

**LTV:CAC :** the amount spent by a customer compared to how much it cost to acquire them. 3/1 is healthy.

**CAC Payback:** The length of time it takes to payback the Customer Acquisition Cost (CAC).
